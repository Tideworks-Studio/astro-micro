> _**Summary:** Trying to do everything yourself stalls your growth and puts your brand quality at risk. The solution is managing your energy, creating systems that reduce operational drag, and setting firm boundaries._

I’m going to make a guess that since you’re here, you’re feeling the ache of the hustle. That feeling that working harder and longer hours is the key to success. I know that this mindset feels like a badge of honor, but it’s the fastest route to burning out, both putting stress on your company and a serious health risk to yourself.

As solopreneurs, we naturally feel protective of our brand. It’s the baby born from our hard work. It’s easy to think that only we know how to approach it the right way, and relinquishing that control is a _ridiculously scary_ thought, but that protectiveness turns into hyper-vigilance quickly, and is more of a risk to your brand than you might realize.

I know this firsthand — when building my brand systems practice, Tideworks Studio, I was very emotionally invested in everything I did _and_ didn’t put out, and my lack of systems caused me to crash. Total exhaustion hit fast. I didn’t know how to manage my energy, and felt the need to be “on” 24/7.

It was obvious that I had hit a wall. My creative output went to zero, and I had no idea how to get it back, but more importantly, _how to retain it once I did_. After a few rounds of this burnout cycle, I learned one of the most important lessons of running a business: **learning to manage your energy and where to invest your time is crucial.**

## The price we pay in burnout-mode

Operating a business from a place of consistent burnout has a fun way of becoming a domino effect of sacrifice.

When you push through while on empty, your brand becomes diluted. Your attention is spread too thin because your body is in survival mode, meanwhile the creative energy well dries up and your work becomes inconsistent.

The lack of strategically placed energy can also lead to damage to your positioning, undercharging to secure a deal, or worse: saying yes to a bad-fit client because you need the work. It’s a vicious cycle of work that drains you, and you’re ultimately sacrificing both yourself for your work, and your work for yourself.

## How burnout manifests in business

_I wish_ I could sit here and tell you that burnout is the same as being tired, and that the answer is simply to rest. Unfortunately, you and I both know that isn’t the case.

If you’re feeling drained at the start of the week, detached from your work, or accomplishments don’t feel as satisfying, that my friend, is a tell-tale sign you’re burnt out.

The thing consuming your time and draining your energy is **operational drag**, the resistance caused by inefficient processes like data entry, inbox fatigue, and constant context-switching. Operational drag is what’s keeping you from truly recovering. So what do we do about it?

We start by looking at ground one. Understand what tasks are taking your energy, define where your priorities lie, and create systems that allow you to scale without the overwhelm.

## The framework to creating efficient systems

### **1. Audit your energy levels**

Grab a pen and paper, and take a look at the things you do every day to move operations forward. This can be as simple as walking yourself through everything necessary to complete a project in your own head. Write down 3–5 things that you feel cause you the most stress or overwhelm within these processes. Note what else before and after is affected by these processes.

### **2. Creating/refining systems**

Take the processes we uncovered in step one, and create a simple checklist for each one. This can be as detailed as you want, but understanding what it takes to get from beginning to end for each process is what’s important here.

For me, I found that it was more effective for me to make sell-sheets for my services instead of over-explaining them in each email. I also switched to using client portals for transparency and handoff software so my clients had easy access to all deliverables well after the project had concluded. I also took a close look at how my sales emails operated, and tried to understand what info I needed to convey and what questions I could anticipate answering to help guide my client through the customer journey.

Take this step-by-step as things come along in order to pace your energy. At the end, you’ll have simple frameworks to follow so you no longer have to work aimlessly.

### **3. Set boundaries (especially with yourself)**

One of the most important aspects of managing your energy is organizing your focus. Settings clear boundaries protects your time and energy.

If you find yourself working at all hours of the day and feel restless, try setting your own working hours and not checking emails or working on projects outside of said hours.

If you’re finding you spend your days switching between all sorts of tasks, and it’s keeping you from getting into a flow state, try batching your work by dedicating specific blocks of time to a single category. This can look like scheduling Tuesday mornings for content writing, or Fridays for financial admin.

Keep trying things until you find a system and schedule that works for you. That’s what’s most important.

## Where to go from here

**Unlink your self-worth from the number of hours you work.** Your energy is your most important asset, and not managing it properly keeps you from running an efficient, scalable business. The truth we have to accept is that we can’t give everything our 100% all hours of the day, _and frankly, we shouldn’t want to._

Identify the things that don’t cause you stress and create systems or hire support for everything else. Take the pressure off, and make a commitment to protecting your energy and health.
